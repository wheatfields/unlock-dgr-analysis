Unlock DGR — Actuarial Hackathon 2026

Data Sources & Analytical Framework

A plain-language overview of the data assembled to support the Unlock DGR campaign — prepared for the 2026 Actuarial Hackathon
The analytical team has assembled a linked dataset drawn from four entirely public sources — the ACNC charity register, the Australian Business Register, the ACNC Annual Information Statements, and ATO Taxation Statistics. This document explains what each source contains, how they were matched together, what the resulting outputs look like, and where the known limitations lie. It is intended for readers familiar with the charity sector who want to understand the evidence base before working with the data.

1The Four Data Sources

ACNC Charity Register

Maintained by the Australian Charities and Not-for-profits Commission (ACNC)

The official public register of every charity registered in Australia. It records each charity's legal name, ABN, registered address, size classification, financial year end, charitable purposes (e.g. advancing health, promoting human rights), and the beneficiary groups it serves. This is the foundation of the analysis — every other data source is matched back to it.

65,430 registered charities
Updated continuously
Extract: May 2026

Australian Business Register — DGR Status

Maintained by the Australian Taxation Office (ATO)

The ABR records whether a charity holds current Deductible Gift Recipient endorsement, and from what date. There is no single downloadable list of all DGR charities, so we queried the register individually for each of the 65,430 charities using their ABN. This produced a definitive DGR yes/no flag and endorsement start date for every charity in the register.

27,623 DGR-endorsed (42%)
37,807 not DGR-endorsed (58%)

ACNC Annual Information Statements (AIS)

Reported by charities to the ACNC; published annually

Each registered charity (other than basic religious charities) submits an Annual Information Statement to the ACNC. The financial section — covering income, donations received, government grants, expenses, assets, and liabilities — is the primary source for comparing the financial health and donation dependency of DGR versus non-DGR charities. Four reporting years (2021–2024) are combined into a harmonised multi-year panel. A companion programs dataset records what each charity actually does, classified against the CLASSIE taxonomy.

211,895 charity-year records
59,197 charities
Reporting years: 2021–2024

ATO Taxation Statistics — Charitable Giving

Published by the Australian Taxation Office, 2023-24 edition

Four statistical tables from the ATO's annual Taxation Statistics publication. The first provides 44 years of national data on tax-deductible gifts claimed by individuals (1978-79 to 2022-23). The second breaks 2022-23 giving down by donor age, sex, and income band — allowing analysis of who claims deductions and at what tax rate. The third counts DGR endorsements by category. The fourth covers Private Ancillary Funds (PAFs) and Public Ancillary Funds (PuAFs) annually from 2000-01 to 2023-24, including assets, inflows, and distributions to DGR recipients.

$9.1B in gifts claimed 2022-23
$13.3B PAF net assets (2023-24)
$4.3B PuAF net assets (2023-24)

2How the Data Was Brought Together

1

Started with the ACNC register as the foundation

Every charity in Australia has a unique 11-digit Australian Business Number (ABN). This ABN is the common reference number that appears across all government databases — similar to how a tax file number links records across systems. The ACNC register provided the complete list of 65,430 charities; all subsequent data was matched to this list using the ABN.

2

Confirmed DGR status for every charity via the ABR

Using each charity's ABN, we queried the Australian Business Register to check current DGR endorsement status and the date from which it was effective. This lookup was performed individually for all 65,430 charities — there is no bulk download available. The result is a confirmed DGR yes/no and endorsement date for every charity in the register.

3

Attached each charity's financial returns

The ACNC Annual Information Statement data for reporting years 2021–2024 was matched to each charity using the ABN, adding income, expenses, and balance sheet figures — 211,895 charity-year records covering 59,197 of the 65,430 charities. Charities with no financial data are typically very small (under $250k in revenue and exempt from financial reporting), newly registered, or basic religious charities with reduced reporting obligations.

4

Identified the four campaign target charity types

Charities were assigned to Justice Connect's four campaign priority cohorts using three complementary evidence sources: the charity's ACNC-declared purposes, its program-level CLASSIE activity classifications from the AIS, and a small set of human-reviewed name rules. Every rule is recorded in an auditable rules file, and only whitelisted rules contribute to the final cohorts. The four cohorts (frozen July 2026) are: neighbourhood houses (312), human rights charities (851), injury prevention charities (237), and disaster preparedness charities (387) — 1,745 distinct charities. These supersede the earlier purpose-plus-name-keyword counts (259 / 847 / 86 / 79), which undercounted because program-level activity data was not yet in use.

4Known Limitations

  • The four campaign cohort counts rely on charities' self-declared ACNC purposes and self-classified program activities; organisations doing this work under broader classifications may still be missed, and a small number of rules-based matches may be false positives. The rules file is auditable and human-reviewed.
  • Financial data in the Annual Information Statements is self-reported by charities and not independently audited at that level. Smaller charities (under $250k in revenue) report less financial detail, so comparisons across size bands require care.
  • The AIS financial panel covers reporting years 2021–2024. Column definitions are harmonised across vintages via an explicit mapping, but reporting practice can still vary year to year.
  • The state field in the ACNC register has minor formatting inconsistencies in the source data. State-level breakdowns should be treated as approximate pending normalisation.
  • The ATO giving statistics capture only tax-deductible donations claimed by individual taxpayers in their returns. They do not include donations not claimed as deductions, corporate charitable giving, or bequests processed outside the tax system — so total philanthropic giving to the sector is higher than these figures suggest.
  • DGR status reflects endorsement as at the time of the ABR lookup (May 2026). Charities that gained or lost DGR status recently may not reflect their status during the financial years covered by the AIS data.
This analysis draws entirely on public sources. Full technical documentation — including data source URLs, processing steps, and field-level definitions for every variable — is available in the project's data dictionary and pipeline map documents.
